Discover the Flavors of Peru

Peruvian cuisine is known for its rich flavors, diverse ingredients, and unique fusion of culinary traditions. From the coastal ceviche to the highland pachamanca, Peru offers a gastronomic experience like no other. Join us as we explore the delightful world of Peruvian food and drinks.

Popular Dishes

Ceviche

Ceviche is a refreshing and zesty dish made from fresh raw fish marinated in citrus juices, usually lime or lemon. The acidity from the citrus juice cooks the fish, giving it a unique texture and flavor. It is typically mixed with onions, cilantro, and chili peppers and served with corn and sweet potatoes. Ceviche is a staple of coastal cuisine in Peru and is often enjoyed as a light, flavorful starter.

Lomo Saltado

Lomo Saltado is a popular Peruvian dish that showcases the fusion of Peruvian and Chinese culinary traditions. It is a delicious stir-fry made with marinated strips of beef, onions, tomatoes, and other ingredients, typically served with fried potatoes and rice. The beef is usually cooked with soy sauce and vinegar, giving it a savory and tangy flavor. This dish is a perfect representation of the cultural melting pot that is Peruvian cuisine.

Arroz con Mariscos

Arroz con Mariscos is a flavorful rice dish similar to paella, featuring a variety of seafood such as shrimp, squid, and mussels. The rice is cooked with seafood broth, garlic, onions, tomatoes, and a mix of spices, creating a rich and aromatic meal. This dish is a favorite along the Peruvian coast and is often enjoyed with a squeeze of lime and a sprinkle of fresh cilantro.

Papa a la Huancaina

Papa a la Huancaina is a classic Peruvian appetizer made from boiled yellow potatoes covered in a creamy, spicy sauce made from queso fresco (fresh cheese), aji amarillo (yellow chili peppers), garlic, milk, and oil. The dish is typically garnished with olives and hard-boiled eggs. It is named after the Huancayo region in the central highlands of Peru and is enjoyed across the country.

Juane

Juane is a traditional dish from the Peruvian Amazon region. It consists of rice, chicken, olives, hard-boiled eggs, and spices, all wrapped in banana leaves and boiled. The dish is often enjoyed during the feast of San Juan, a major celebration in the Amazon. The unique cooking method and the infusion of flavors from the banana leaves give Juane its distinct taste and aroma.

Aji de Gallina

Aji de Gallina is a comforting and creamy chicken stew made with shredded chicken, aji amarillo (yellow chili peppers), walnuts, cheese, and milk. The dish is usually served over rice and accompanied by boiled potatoes and olives. It has a rich and slightly spicy flavor, making it a favorite in many Peruvian households. The combination of ingredients results in a unique and hearty meal that is both flavorful and satisfying.

Traditional Desserts

Arroz con Leche

Arroz con Leche is a classic Peruvian dessert similar to rice pudding. It is made from rice cooked in milk with cinnamon, cloves, and sugar, often garnished with raisins and a sprinkle of cinnamon on top. This creamy and sweet dessert is a favorite among Peruvians and is enjoyed warm or cold, offering a comforting end to any meal.

Mazamorra Morada

Mazamorra Morada is a traditional Peruvian dessert made from purple corn, which gives it its distinctive color. The dessert is a thick, sweet pudding flavored with cinnamon, cloves, and fruit like pineapple and quince. It is often enjoyed on its own or mixed with Arroz con Leche in a popular combination known as "combinado."

Picarones

Picarones are delicious Peruvian doughnuts made from a dough of squash and sweet potatoes, deep-fried until golden brown. They are typically served with a syrup made from chancaca, a type of raw sugar, flavored with cinnamon and cloves. These sweet, crispy treats are often enjoyed as a dessert or snack, especially during festivals and street fairs.

Traditional Drinks

Chicha Morada

Chicha Morada is a popular non-alcoholic beverage in Peru, made from purple corn, pineapple, cinnamon, and cloves, sweetened with sugar. The drink is boiled and then chilled, resulting in a refreshing and antioxidant-rich beverage. It is often served during meals and is enjoyed for its unique flavor and health benefits.

Pisco Sour

The Pisco Sour is the national drink of Peru, made with Pisco (a type of brandy), lime juice, simple syrup, egg white, and a dash of Angostura bitters. The cocktail is shaken to create a frothy top and served chilled. Known for its balanced sweet and sour flavors, the Pisco Sour is a must-try for anyone visiting Peru.

Inca Kola

Inca Kola, often referred to as "the Golden Kola," is a bright yellow soda with a unique, sweet flavor that is immensely popular in Peru. Despite its name, it does not taste like traditional cola beverages but has a taste more akin to bubblegum or cream soda. It is commonly enjoyed with meals and holds a special place in Peruvian culture.

Top Restaurants in Peru

Central (Lima): Ranked among the world's best restaurants, Central offers a unique dining experience that takes you through Peru's diverse ecosystems. Each dish is meticulously crafted to reflect different altitudes and regions of Peru, providing an immersive culinary journey.

Maido (Lima): Famous for its Nikkei cuisine, Maido blends Japanese and Peruvian flavors in a way that delights the palate. The restaurant is renowned for its innovative dishes and exceptional presentation, offering a fusion dining experience like no other.

Astrid y Gastón (Lima): This iconic restaurant, led by renowned chef Gastón Acurio, serves traditional Peruvian dishes with a modern twist. The menu is a celebration of Peruvian ingredients and culinary techniques, presented in a sophisticated and elegant setting.

La Rosa Nautica (Lima): A seafood lover's paradise, La Rosa Nautica is known for its outstanding ceviche and other seafood dishes. Situated on a pier overlooking the Pacific Ocean, the restaurant offers not only delicious food but also breathtaking views.
